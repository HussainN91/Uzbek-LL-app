### THE CONTRASTIVE MECHANISM

A Contrastive Turn is a short 2-turn exchange between two characters that highlights a structural difference (e.g., singular vs. plural, present vs. past, affirmative vs. negative).

**Structure:**

## Grammar Structures

### Main Grammar Point: `Present Simple (Affirmative) + Adverbs of Frequency`

#### Pattern 1: Simple Routine (I/You/We/They)
- **I + verb + time phrase**: "I wake up at seven", "I have breakfast at home"
- **You + verb + time phrase**: "You leave the house at eight"
- **We + verb + time phrase**: "We go to school every day"
- **They + verb + time phrase**: "They play football on weekends"

#### Pattern 2: Simple Routine (He/She)
- **He/She + verb+s + time phrase**: "He wakes up early", "She works in an office"
- **My friend + verb+s + time phrase**: "My mother wakes up early", "My father eats dinner"

#### Pattern 3: Adverbs of Frequency
- **I usually/often + verb**: "I usually wake up early"
- **I always/never + verb**: "I always clean my teeth"
- **Every day/On weekends + I + verb**: "Every day I go to school"
- **Subject + verb + adverb**: "She always studies English"
- **Subject + be + adverb + adjective/place**: "She is always happy", "He is usually at home"

### Secondary Grammar Point: `Present Simple Negative & Questions (do/does/don't/doesn't)`

#### Pattern 4: Negative Statements (I/You/We/They)
- **I don't + verb**: "I don't wake up early"
- **You don't + verb**: "You don't work on weekends"
- **We don't + verb**: "We don't play football"
- **They don't + verb**: "They don't study at night"

#### Pattern 5: Negative Statements (He/She/It)
- **He/She doesn't + verb**: "He doesn't wake up early", "She doesn't work on weekends"
- **My father doesn't + verb**: "My father doesn't play football"

#### Pattern 6: Yes/No Questions (I/You/We/They)
- **Do + I/you/we/they + verb?**: "Do you wake up early?", "Do they work on weekends?"
- **Short answers:** "Yes, I do / No, I don't"

#### Pattern 7: Yes/No Questions (He/She/It)
- **Does + he/she/it + verb?**: "Does he wake up early?", "Does she work on weekends?"
- **Short answers:** "Yes, he does / No, he doesn't"

### Tertiary Grammar Point: `Present Continuous (be + verb-ing)`

#### Pattern 8: Actions Happening Now (I/You/We/They)
- **I am + verb-ing**: "I am reading now"
- **You are + verb-ing**: "You are watching TV"
- **We are + verb-ing**: "We are playing games"
- **They are + verb-ing**: "They are studying now"

#### Pattern 9: Actions Happening Now (He/She)
- **He/She is + verb-ing**: "He is reading now", "She is watching TV"
- **My brother is + verb-ing**: "My brother is playing games"

#### Time Marker (Now)
- **now**: Used with present continuous to show current action

### Present Simple Verb Forms

#### Base Form (I/You/We/They)
- wake, have, get, leave, go, play, work, study, watch, read, eat, drink

#### Third Person Singular (He/She/It/Singular noun)
- wakes, has, gets, leaves, goes, plays, works, studies, watches, reads, eats, drinks

## Grammar Structures

### Main Grammar Point: `Past Simple (Regular Verbs) - Add -ed`

#### Pattern 1: Affirmative Statements
- **Subject + verb-ed (+ time phrase)**: "I played football yesterday"
- **I/You/We/They + verb-ed**: "I walked to school", "They visited the museum"
- **He/She/It + verb-ed**: "She watched TV", "He worked at the office"

#### Pattern 2: Regular Verb Conjugation
- Add **-ed** to most verbs: play â†’ played, walk â†’ walked, watch â†’ watched
- Verbs ending in 'y': study â†’ studied, try â†’ tried, carry â†’ carried
- Double final consonant: stop â†’ stopped, plan â†’ planned

#### Pattern 3: Time Markers with Past Simple
- **Yesterday** (kecha): "I played football yesterday"
- **Last week/month** (o'tgan hafta/oy): "We visited the museum last week"
- **Time phrase at end of sentence**: "I walked home after school"

#### Pattern 4: Sequencing Past Actions
- **Subject + verb-ed + then + subject + verb-ed**: "I worked, then I walked home"
- **After + gerund + subject + verb-ed**: "After dinner, I watched TV"

### Secondary Grammar Point: `Past Simple Negative & Questions (did/didn't)`

#### Pattern 5: Negative Statements (All Subjects)
- **Subject + didn't + base verb (NOT verb-ed)**: "I didn't go to school", "She didn't play football"
- **Important:** Use BASE VERB after didn't, not -ed form
- âœ… Correct: "I didn't play" âŒ Wrong: "I didn't played"

#### Pattern 6: Yes/No Questions (All Subjects)
- **Did + subject + base verb?**: "Did you go?", "Did she visit?", "Did they play?"
- **Did + subject + base verb + complement?**: "Did you play football yesterday?"
- **Important:** Use BASE VERB after did, not -ed form
- âœ… Correct: "Did you play?" âŒ Wrong: "Did you played?"

#### Pattern 7: Short Answers
- **Yes, + subject + did**: "Yes, I did", "Yes, she did"
- **No, + subject + didn't**: "No, I didn't", "No, she didn't"

#### Pattern 8: Connectors - and, but, or (NEW - ADVERBIAL PROGRESSION)
- **Sentence + and + sentence**: "I played football **and** I was tired"
- **Sentence + but + sentence**: "I worked **but** I wasn't tired"
- **Question + or + question**: "Did you play **or** did you study?"

**Connector Usage:**
- **and** (va) = Addition: Both things are true
  - "I visited the museum and I bought souvenirs"
- **but** (lekin/ammo) = Contrast: Unexpected result
  - "I played all day but I wasn't tired"
- **or** (yoki) = Alternative: One or the other
  - "Do you like pizza or burgers?"

#### Pattern 9: Company Adverbials - with (NEW - ADVERBIAL PROGRESSION)
- **Subject + verb-ed + with + person**: "I played **with my friends**"
- **Subject + verb-ed + with + possessive + noun**: "She walked **with her sister**"
- **Subject + verb-ed + alone**: "I studied **alone**"

- **Subject + didn't work as + [job]**: "I didn't work as a dentist", "She didn't work as a model"
- **Short answers**: "Yes, I did work as a farmer" / "No, I didn't work as an engineer"

#### Important Grammar Rule
- With **did/didn't**, always use the **base verb**, NOT the -ed form
- âœ… Correct: "Did you play?" / "I didn't play"
- âŒ Wrong: "Did you played?" / "I didn't played"

- **Subject + verb + adverb + to + place**: "I walked **quickly** to the museum"
- **Subject + verb + adverb + time**: "She ate **quickly** yesterday"

**Manner Adverb Formation:**
Most manner adverbs are formed by adding **-ly** to an adjective:
- slow â†’ slowly, quick â†’ quickly, careful â†’ carefully
- Exception: fast â†’ fast (no change), hard â†’ hard (no change)

**Manner Adverbs Vocabulary:**

- **fast** (tez): "He ran fast to catch the bus"

**Adverb Position:**
- After the verb: "I walked **slowly**"
- Before "to + place": "I walked **slowly** to school"
- End of sentence: "She finished her work **quickly**"

- "I walked **slowly** **to the museum** **yesterday**" (manner + direction + time)

### GUIDELINE G2: Thinking Pressure (Mastery Dialogue Only)

**Purpose:** In Stage 3.1 (Mastery Peak / Linguistic Pressure dialogues), force automaticity through time pressure.

| Parameter | Value | Description |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| `VANISHING_CUE_MS` | 800 | Text fades to 0% opacity after audio playback starts |
| `RESPONSE_DEADLINE` | 2000 | 2.0s timer for student to initiate production |

**Implementation Rules:**
- Applies ONLY to the final mastery pass (Dialogue X.1), NOT during initial learning
- If user fails to respond within `RESPONSE_DEADLINE`, the turn resets (no penalty, just retry)
- Targets the Basal Ganglia for stress-free retrieval under mild time pressure
- Text must move to `opacity: 0` (not `display: none`) to prevent Rote Reading
- Visual timer: progress bar that depletes over 2.0s

**Cognitive Rationale:** The 800ms vanishing window is calibrated to allow initial phonological encoding but prevent visual crutch-reading. The 2.0s response deadline creates productive struggle without anxiety.
